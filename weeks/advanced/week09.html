Timber Conversion: From Logs to Lumber

Now let‚Äôs switch to theory: understanding how the timber we use is produced from logs. Timber conversion refers to the process of sawing logs into planks and boards. The method of conversion affects the wood‚Äôs grain and stability:

  • Through and Through (Plain Sawing): The log is sliced straight through repeatedly to make boards. This is the most economical method and produces boards quickly with minimal repositioning of the log. It results in a mix of grain orientations ‚Äì some boards will have near-vertical grain (toward the centre of the log), others will have wide arcs (near the outer parts). Plain-sawn boards often have a distinctive ‚Äúcathedral‚Äù grain pattern on the face. They tend to be prone to cupping as they season, because of growth ring curvature. However, this method yields maximum wood with least waste and is commonly used for construction lumber.
  • Quarter Sawing: The log is first cut into quarters, and then boards are sawn from each quarter at angles. This method yields boards where growth rings are more perpendicular to the face. Quarter-sawn boards have a straighter grain appearance and often show attractive figuring (like oak‚Äôs ‚Äúflecks‚Äù). They are more dimensionally stable (less warping, less expansion and contraction across the width). The downside is quarter sawing is time-consuming, wasteful of material, and thus more expensive.
  • Rift Sawing: A variation aiming for very vertical grain in each board. It yields very consistent (but more narrow) boards, often used in fine furniture for uniform grain. This wastes even more wood than quarter sawing and is rarely done except for speciality needs.

Most wood you buy will be plain-sawn unless it‚Äôs specifically advertised as quarter-sawn. For example, our pine boards likely come from through-and-through sawing. You might notice the grain patterns and any cupping or bowing in them ‚Äì now you know why those characteristics occur.

Why does this matter? As a woodworker, if you know a board is cut a certain way, you can predict how it will behave. Quarter-sawn pieces for frame parts might resist warping better over years. Plain-sawn pieces might need extra care in finishing to seal them to reduce moisture changes. It also explains cost differences: quarter-sawn timber, being more labour-intensive and with more waste, costs more.

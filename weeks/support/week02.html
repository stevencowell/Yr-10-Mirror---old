Week 2: Design & Sketches, Measuring & Marking Out

Introduction

Week 2 focuses on the design process and the fundamental skills of measuring and marking out timber accurately. A good design starts with clear sketches, and quality construction begins with precise measurements.

Design and Sketching

Start by sketching your mirror frame ideas. Consider shape, style, and any decorative elements. Your design sketches should include dimensions and notes. Think about how each piece of the frame joins together. This is also the time to apply design principles like balance and proportion, and to ensure your concept meets the project constraints (from Week 1).

Once you have a draft sketch, draw a more detailed plan (to scale if possible). Label all parts. This drawing will guide your cutting and assembly in later weeks.

Measuring and Marking Out

Accurate measuring and marking is critical. “Measure twice, cut once” is the golden rule. Key tools for marking out include the tape measure, ruler, try square, and marking gauge. Some tips for precision:

  • Use a sharp pencil or marking knife for fine lines. A blunt pencil can lead to a mark that’s 1–2 mm thick, introducing error.
  • When using a ruler or tape, keep your eye directly above the measurement to avoid parallax error (misreading from an angle).
  • For repeat measurements, use story sticks or templates to ensure consistency rather than measuring afresh each time.
  • Mark clearly but lightly on timber; heavy marks can dent softwood or be hard to remove.

Safe Use of Tools

As you mark and cut timber, always practise tool safety. For example, when using a tenon saw or hand saw, secure the wood in a vice or with clamps, and keep your free hand clear of the blade path. Use a bench hook for sawing small pieces. For marking with an awl or knife, keep your supporting hand behind the cutting edge. These habits prevent accidents during the basic but essential step of material preparation.

Conclusion

By combining creative design with precise measuring and marking, you have laid out a roadmap for your project. A well-drawn plan and accurate marks on timber will make the upcoming cutting and joining steps much smoother.

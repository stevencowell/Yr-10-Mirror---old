Week 5: Mortise & Tenon Joints (Part 1)

Introduction

Mortise and tenon joints are among the most traditional and reliable woodworking joints, often dubbed the ‚Äúking of joints‚Äù for their strength. In Weeks 5 and 6, we will learn how to create a mortise and tenon (M&T) joint and explore variations or alternative joints if applicable. This week (Part 1), we focus on the basics: understanding the joint and cutting the mortise.

Mortise and Tenon Basics

A mortise and tenon joint connects two pieces of wood at a right angle, commonly used in frame construction (like table legs and rails, or in our case, potentially the mirror frame corners if done as bridle joints or reinforced corners). The mortise is the rectangular cavity cut into one piece, and the tenon is the corresponding tongue cut on the end of the other piece that fits into the mortise.

Key characteristics of a good M&T joint:

  • The tenon shoulders (the parts that sit flush against the mortised piece) should fit tightly with no gaps.
  • The tenon should fill the mortise with minimal wiggle, but not so tight that it needs to be forced (glue needs a bit of space).
  • Mortise depth is usually a bit more than the tenon length so excess glue has somewhere to go (often tenon is about 3-5mm shorter than mortise depth to avoid bottoming out).
  • Typically, the mortise width is about one-third the thickness of the workpiece.

Marking and Cutting the Mortise

We will cut mortises by hand using drills and chisels (since dedicated mortising machines are limited). Steps to create a mortise:

  1. Layout: On the piece of wood that will receive the mortise (e.g., a frame stile), clearly mark the outline of the mortise. Use a square and marking gauge to ensure accuracy. For instance, if our frame pieces are 19mm thick, a common mortise might be ~6mm wide (1/3 thickness) and perhaps 30mm deep. Mark the mortise location centred on the thickness and at the appropriate distance from the end (depending on design).
  2. Drilling (Waste Removal): Secure the wood. Using a drill press or handheld drill with a brad-point bit of appropriate size (slightly less width than the mortise), drill a series of overlapping holes within the mortise outline. Drill to the required depth. This removes most of the waste wood.
  3. Chiselling: Use sharp bench chisels to square up the mortise. Start by chiselling along the ends (the cross-grain sides) of the mortise to define the edges, then work from each end toward the middle to remove remaining waste. Keep the chisel vertical and use a mallet for controlled force. Periodically test the depth and clean out chips. The result should be straight, flat mortise walls.
  4. Test Fit (with scrap tenon): If possible, have a scrap piece cut to the mortise width to test the fit. It should slide in with hand pressure and not wobble side-to-side.

Note: Precision is crucial. A mortise that is too narrow can be widened with careful paring, but one that‚Äôs too wide will make the joint sloppy. Take your time with layout and chiselling.

Tools and Safety for Mortising

While chopping a mortise, always keep hands behind the chisel cutting edge. Secure your workpiece with clamps or in a vice ‚Äì never hold the piece in your hand while chiselling. If using a drill, clamp the wood to prevent it spinning if the bit catches. Wear safety glasses especially during chiselling; small wood chips can fly off.

We are using sharp chisels; handle them with respect. When not in use, set them down with the sharp edge away from you and others.

Alternative Joint Consideration

If a traditional mortise and tenon seems too challenging or not suitable for your mirror frame corners, an alternative joint that might be used is a bridle joint (open mortise and tenon) or a reinforced miter (with a spline). We will primarily practise M&T because it teaches transferable skills. However, keep in mind these alternatives for different project needs:

  • Bridle Joint: Essentially a tenon that goes through the end of the piece (open on top), like an open mortise. Easier to cut than a hidden mortise and tenon and useful for frame corners.
  • Spline or Biscuit Reinforced Miter: As learnt, a biscuit or thin spline can reinforce a miter joint on a frame if M&T is not used.

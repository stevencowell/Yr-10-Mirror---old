Week 3: Dressing Timber to Size & The Hand Plane

Introduction

In Week 3, we move from planning to preparing materials. You will dress and prepare timber to size – turning rough stock into the exact dimensions needed for your mirror frame. A key tool this week is the hand plane, and we’ll explore its function in detail.

Dressing the Timber

“Dressing” timber means smoothing and squaring it so that all pieces are true to size. Often, timber as supplied has rough surfaces or slight warps. The process involves:

  • Cutting to Rough Length: Using a saw, cut your timber slightly longer than final lengths for the frame sides. This makes handling easier during planing.
  • Face and Edge Planning: Choose one face of the board to be your reference face. Use a jack plane or jointer plane to flatten it. Then plane one adjacent edge at 90° to that face (use a try square to check squareness). These will be your datum surfaces.
  • Thicknessing: Once one face is flat, you can mark the desired thickness on the edges and plane down the opposite face until the board is of uniform thickness. If available, a thicknesser machine can speed this step (instructor will demonstrate machine use, if applicable).
  • Trimming to Final Dimensions: After planing, saw or plane the pieces to their exact length and width as per your design measurements.

The Function of the Hand Plane

The hand plane is an essential tool for dressing timber. It works by shaving off thin layers of wood, using a sharp blade set at a fixed depth. Key points about hand planes:

  • A sharp blade (iron) is critical – a dull blade will tear the wood instead of shaving it, resulting in a rough finish.
  • Proper adjustment of the blade is important. For fine smoothing, only a very small portion of the blade should protrude.
  • Technique: Plane along the grain whenever possible. Use long, even strokes. Apply pressure on the front knob at the start of the stroke and on the tote (handle) at the end to maintain a level cut.
  • Check flatness frequently by sighting down the board or using a straightedge. Mark high spots with pencil and focus planing there.

Benefits of Accurate Preparation

Taking time to dress your timber correctly pays off later. Straight, square pieces ensure that joints fit tightly and the frame comes out square. It also improves the finish quality; for instance, a planed surface often needs less sanding. Remember, any twist or bow in your pieces now will make joinery difficult later.

Mirror Unit

This unit provides students with the opportunity to gain basic hand skills and confidence in working with timber. Over 20 weeks, students will learn to safely use a range of tools and equipment, master joinery techniques, and document their processes in line with key Industrial Technology outcomes.

Summary & Duration

Duration: 20 Weeks (40-minute lessons, 4 per week)
Focus: Workshop safety, marking & measuring, cutting, joinery, finishing, self-evaluation, and project presentation.

Unit Overview

This ‚ÄúMirror‚Äù unit (Stage 5) focuses on building practical woodworking skills alongside an understanding of safety and design principles. Students use hand, power, and machine tools to construct a mirror frame while meeting both theory and practical assessment criteria.

Learning Outcomes & Assessment

  • IND5-1: identifies, assesses, applies and manages the risks and WHS issues associated with using a range of tools, equipment, materials, processes and technologies.
  • IND5-2: applies design principles in the modification, development and production of projects.
  • IND5-3: identifies, selects and uses a range of hand and machine tools, equipment and processes to produce quality practical projects.
  • IND5-4: selects, justifies and uses a range of relevant and associated materials for specific applications.
  • IND5-5: selects, interprets and applies a range of suitable communication techniques in the development, planning, production and presentation of ideas and projects.
  • IND5-6: identifies and participates in collaborative work practices in the learning environment.
  • IND5-7: applies and transfers skills, processes and materials to a variety of contexts and projects.
  • IND5-8: evaluates products in terms of functional, economic, aesthetic and environmental qualities and quality of construction.
  • IND5-9: describes, analyses and uses a range of current, new and emerging technologies and their various applications.
  • IND5-10: describes, analyses and evaluates the impact of technology on society, the environment and cultural issues locally and globally.

Weekly Breakdown

Week 1: Workshop Introduction, Marking & Measuring Exercise

Content: Introduction to workshop safety, proper use of marking and measuring tools, and understanding WHS signage.

Theory: Onguard safety topics, general workshop protocols, and introduction to WHS signage symbols.

Practical: Guided workshop tour, identifying safety equipment and exits. Demonstration of accurate measuring and marking using tape measures, rulers, and try squares on a practice piece.

Resources: Workshop Safety Handout, Measuring Tools Reference, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 1 Safety Quiz.

Week 2: Design Sketches & Timber Mark-out

Content: Develop mirror frame design sketches meeting given constraints. Practice precise mark-out of timber parts according to design dimensions.

Theory: Design principles (balance, proportion) applied to mirror frames; safe tool use for marking (pencil, marking gauge) and cutting plan overview.

Practical: Students draw at least two concept sketches of their mirror frame. Begin marking timber lengths on stock wood for frame components using their chosen design, double-checking measurements.

Resources: Design Brief with Constraints, Sample Mirror Images for Inspiration, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 2 Design Submission.

Week 3: Timber Dressing & Hand Plane Use

Content: Prepare (dress) timber to correct size: flatten and square faces/edges. Introduction to the FEWTEL method of stock preparation. Emphasis on proper hand plane technique.

Theory: FEWTEL sequence explained. Wood grain and planing with the grain vs. against it, and the function of a jointer vs. thicknesser.

Practical: Students use planes (or machine tools if available) to flatten one face and square one edge of each piece, then mark and cut their pieces to rough width/length. Practice planing and check squareness with try squares and winding sticks.

Resources: Bench Planes (Jack/Smoothing), FEWTEL Cheat Sheet, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 3 FEWTEL Worksheet.

Week 4: Joinery Methods ‚Äì Dowel, Biscuit & Domino

Content: Explore various frame joinery techniques. Hands-on practice with dowel joints and biscuit joints for the mirror frame corners.

Theory: Compare dowel vs. biscuit vs. Festool Domino joinery in terms of strength and application. Consider factors in wood selection (grain, defects).

Practical: Drill and fit test dowel joints on scrap timber. Use a biscuit joiner to cut slots on test pieces. Evaluate alignment and strength. Decide which joinery to use for the project.

Resources: Dowel Jig, Biscuit Joiner, Dowel/Biscuit Stock, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 4 Joinery Comparison Chart.

Week 5: Mortise & Tenon Joinery ‚Äì Part 1

Content: Begin cutting mortise and tenon joints for frame corners (if using this method). Emphasis on accuracy in marking and cutting for a snug fit.

Theory: Anatomy of a mortise and tenon (shoulders, cheeks, etc.) and why it‚Äôs strong. Proper use of chisels and saws; on-guard safety for chiseling.

Practical: Mark out mortises on frame stiles and matching tenons on rails. Start drilling/chiseling out mortises and sawing tenons on two corners. Perform frequent test fits.

Resources: Bevel Edge Chisels, Tenon Saw, Mortise Gauge, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 5 Mortise & Tenon Reflection (with photo upload).

Week 6: Mortise & Tenon Joinery ‚Äì Part 2

Content: Complete all four mortise and tenon joints. Refine fits and troubleshoot any issues (tight/loose joints) across all corners.

Theory: Recap joinery tips; when and how to adjust joints (e.g., paring a tight tenon). Safe use of the bandsaw (if applicable) for cutting tenon cheeks.

Practical: Finish cutting remaining mortises and tenons. Use a bandsaw for tenons if available to increase accuracy. Dry-fit each joint as it‚Äôs completed to ensure the frame will assemble correctly.

Resources: Bandsaw (for demo), Sandpaper for fine tuning, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 6 Joinery Check-in Discussion.

Week 7: Router Profiles & Timber Characteristics

Content: Use a router to add decorative edges (round-over, chamfer) and cut a rebate for the mirror glass. Study different timber types and features.

Theory: Router bit types and safe operation; comparison of softwood and hardwood properties and why pine is used here vs. when hardwood might be preferred.

Practical: Students rout the outer edges of their frame pieces for a smooth finish and the inner back edge to create a step for the glass. Examine frame pieces of different wood samples to note differences.

Resources: Router with Round-over/Rebate Bits, Scrap Wood for Test Routing, Timber Samples Chart, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 7 Router & Timber Quiz.

Week 8: Dry Clamping & Work Method Statements

Content: Dry assemble the mirror frame with clamps to ensure fit and squareness. Develop a simple SWMS for the glue-up and finishing steps.

Theory: Importance of dry-fitting and checking for square by measuring diagonals. Introduction to SWMS ‚Äì identifying potential hazards and controls in assembly and finishing.

Practical: Assemble frame pieces without glue, using clamps to align joints. Adjust clamps for squareness. In groups, complete an SWMS template for the glue-up process.

Resources: Sash/Bar Clamps, Carpenter‚Äôs Square, SWMS Template, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 8 SWMS Draft Submission.

Week 9: Design Features & Timber Conversion

Content: Add any remaining design features (decorative cuts, engravings) to the mirror frame. Discuss timber conversion methods (plain-sawn vs. quarter-sawn) and how they affect wood quality.

Theory: Timber conversion ‚Äì how logs are sawn into boards, the impact on grain and stability, and cost/yield considerations. Also, a discussion on calculating material costs to minimize waste.

Practical: Implement decorative elements on the frame if planned. Optionally complete a spreadsheet exercise on material cost estimation.

Resources: Coping Saw/Files, Laser Cutter (if available), Timber Conversion Diagrams, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 9 Costing & Conversion Activity.

Week 10: Finishing Design & Workshop Maintenance

Content: Finalize design elements on the mirror frame and prepare for the finishing stage. Conduct a group workshop maintenance session.

Theory: Importance of surface preparation before finishing; overview of various wood finishes and their environmental impacts; discussion on maintaining a clean, efficient workshop.

Practical: Sand all frame components to a fine finish. Organize and clean the workshop ‚Äì sweeping, tool storage, and checking that work areas are hazard-free.

Resources: Sandpaper (120/180/240 grit), Finish Samples, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 10 Maintenance Checklist.

Week 11: Frame Assembly & Timber Seasoning

Content: Glue and clamp the mirror frame together. Learn about timber seasoning ‚Äì the process of drying wood and its importance for stability.

Theory: Best practices for gluing (amount of glue, clamp pressure, cleaning squeeze-out). Timber seasoning: differences between green and seasoned wood; air drying vs. kiln drying; impact on wood stability.

Practical: Assemble the frame with glue and clamps. Test for squareness. Use a moisture meter (if available) on scrap wood to compare seasoned vs. unseasoned wood.

Resources: Wood Glue, Clamps, Moisture Meter, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 11 Seasoning Quiz.

Week 12: Final Assembly & Timber Milling

Content: Unclamp and inspect the glued frame. Discuss industrial timber milling processes and how they compare to our manual preparation.

Theory: Overview of sawmill operations ‚Äì jointing, planing, rip and crosscut; environmental considerations in milling; efficient material usage.

Practical: Remove clamps, clean up excess glue with a chisel, and test the strength of each joint. Watch a brief sawmill tour video and discuss how our processes mimic those in industry.

Resources: Cabinet Scraper/Chisel, Sawmill Video, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 12 Sawmill Reflection Prompt.

Week 13: Finishing Techniques & Environmental Sustainability

Content: Begin the finishing process: sanding and applying the first coat of finish. Discuss various finish types and sustainable woodworking practices.

Theory: Overview of oil finishes, polyurethane varnish, shellac, and wax. Advantages and disadvantages of each; sustainable practices such as using low-VOC products and responsibly sourced timber.

Practical: Sand the assembled frame with fine grit; apply the first coat of your chosen finish under safe conditions. Complete a sustainability worksheet discussing eco-friendly practices in woodworking.

Resources: Finishing Oil/Polyurethane, Nitrile Gloves, Sanding Block, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 13 Sustainable Wood Discussion.

Week 14: Glass Installation & Industry Insights

Content: Install mirror glass into the finished frame. Connect classroom learning with industry practices through an industry visit or virtual tour.

Theory: Glass handling and installation methods (using mirror clips, glazier‚Äôs points, or beading). Discussion on professional woodworking practices and technologies (e.g., CNC, automated joinery, large-scale finishing).

Practical: Carefully fit the mirror glass into the frame‚Äôs rebate using appropriate tools (with teacher supervision). Attach hanging hardware if required. Complete an industry insight worksheet detailing one observation from the visit or video.

Resources: Pre-cut Mirror Glass, Glass Cutter, Glazier‚Äôs Points, Hanging Hardware, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 14 Industry Link Worksheet.

Week 15: Safe Handling of Finishes & MSDS

Content: Focus on chemical safety: handling wood finishes, solvents, and adhesives properly. Learn to read a Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) for a common finishing product.

Theory: Identify hazards of chemicals (flammability, toxicity) and understand the structure of an MSDS (Identification, Hazards, Composition, First Aid, Firefighting, Accidental Release, Handling & Storage, Exposure Controls, Disposal). Emphasize key safety measures.

Practical: Examine an MSDS (provided on Classroom) for a wood finish. Answer questions regarding PPE recommendations and safe storage/disposal. Perform a safety drill on what to do in case of a chemical spill.

Resources: MSDS Printout for Wood Finish, Chemical Hazard Symbol Chart, Metal Disposal Container, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 15 Chemical Safety Quiz.

Week 16: Polishing & Sustainability Review

Content: Apply final polish or touch-ups to the mirror frame. Reflect on sustainable practices throughout the project.

Theory: Polishing techniques (using wax, buffing, alternatives like rubbing compound). Review sustainable woodworking practices: renewable timber, low-VOC finishes, minimal waste, and upcycling. How these choices impact both quality and environmental footprint.

Practical: Polish the frame using furniture wax or a similar product. Complete a sustainability reflection assignment discussing one sustainable practice you applied.

Resources: Furniture Wax, Buffing Cloth, Steel Wool, Google Classroom ‚Äì Week 16 Sustainability Reflection.

Weeks 1 & 2: Workshop Safety and Timber Marking

Theory

Always follow workshop safety rules. Wear safety glasses and strong shoes. Keep the workshop clean and tell your teacher if you see damaged tools. When marking timber, use a pencil and ruler or marking gauge to draw clear lines. Always measure carefully before cutting.

Weeks 3 & 4: Timber Dressing and Simple Joints

Theory

"Dressing timber" means making wood flat and smooth. A hand plane is used to remove bumps. Always plane timber in the same direction as the grain to prevent tearing the wood. Simple timber joints like dowel joints use small wooden pins to join pieces strongly.

Weeks 5 & 6: Mortise and Tenon Joints

Theory

Mortise and tenon joints are strong timber joints. A mortise is a rectangular hole, and a tenon is the piece of wood that fits into it. Always use a sharp chisel safely to cut mortises. Test fit the joint before adding glue.

Weeks 7 & 8: Router Use and Timber Properties

Theory

A router cuts decorative edges or grooves in wood. It spins very fast and must be used carefully. Timber types matter: pine (softwood) is lighter and easier to cut, while oak (hardwood) is heavier and tougher.

Weeks 9 & 10: Timber Conversion and Workshop Maintenance

Theory

Timber conversion is how logs are cut into boards. Quarter-sawn timber is cut into quarters to make boards more stable. Cleaning the workshop regularly keeps it safe and organised.

Theory

Timber seasoning means drying wood to remove moisture. Seasoned timber stays straight and does not bend. Frames are glued and clamped to ensure they are strong and square.

Timber finishing protects the wood and makes it look nice. Common finishes are oil, wax, or varnish. Sustainability means using wood wisely and protecting forests by reducing waste.
